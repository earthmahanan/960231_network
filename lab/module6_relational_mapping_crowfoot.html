⚠️ Common mistake

Don't forget attributes that belong to the relationship itself (like an actor's role name, or a screening date) — these live in the bridge table, not in either entity table.

📌 Task

Unlike Exercises 1–3, you are not mapping a given ERD — you are designing one from scratch based on the business description below, using Crow's Foot notation (not Chen notation).

Scenario · Sales Departments
Design the ER model (Crow's Foot style) of the given description:
  • The system must be able to record the details of each PRODUCT, i.e. product code, product name, the DEPARTMENT that the product has been sold, and the contact information of the SUPPLIER.
  • The INVOICES have to be issued to the CUSTOMER for product selling. An invoice's detail includes invoice ID, customer code/name, date/time of issuing, total amount to be paid (by the customer), and the detail of each product in such invoice including product code, product name, quantity, and amount of money to be paid by the customer for such product.
  • The price of products can be changed through the time. Such change has been proceeded at the end of the day (23:50–0:00), so during a single day, the price has to be fixed. The system must be able to report the history of such changes.
  • The system must allow changes for customers' details.
  • Availability of each product, i.e. total remaining and each remaining from each purchasing, must be able to be reported.
Step 1 · Build the ER diagram on the canvas (Crow's Foot)
Q4.1 & Q4.2 Drag Entity boxes onto the canvas, add attributes (tick the checkbox for PK), then turn on Connect mode and click two entities in a row to draw a relationship — pick the Crow's Foot cardinality symbol for each side and give the relationship a verb-phrase label. Click the ✎ badge on any line later to edit or delete it.
🛒
Why this matters: this is basically a mini point-of-sale schema — the same shape used by Shopify, Square, and every retail ERP on the planet. Price history and per-batch inventory are exactly the two things that separate a toy schema from one that survives an audit.
